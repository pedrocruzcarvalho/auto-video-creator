# Fern / Blackfiles-Inspired Documentary Style Bible

Use this as a practical art-direction brief, not as a request to copy any one channel exactly.

## Core Format

- The first 60-90 seconds are usually an intro only: establish date, place, impossible problem, main character, stakes, and a sharp question.
- Long videos use chapters after the intro. Chapter cards are not read by the narrator.
- Chapters should feel like title cards in a dark control room: flicker, scanlines, low light, green/yellow/red glowing text, and a short glitch or camera-click sound.
- Each chapter is roughly 4-6 minutes in a long video. Do not add chapters to a 60-120 second test unless explicitly requested.
- Act as the full creative director: script, shot selection, movement, evidence inserts, overlay text, overlay placement, dimming, sound cues, ambience, and transitions must all feel intentional.
- The final edit should feel expensive and authored, not like a list of generated clips.

## Intro Structure

- Start with a concrete date when historically feasible.
- Immediately place the viewer in a location.
- Introduce the power imbalance: one person or small group versus a state, corporation, prison, army, court, agency, or impossible system.
- Give a surprising detail within the first 20 seconds.
- End the intro with a question that makes the viewer need the rest of the video.

Example rhythm, not exact wording:

- Date.
- Location.
- People in a room.
- Main character introduced.
- Impossible legal/technical/political move.
- Past impossible achievement.
- Future cost or consequence.
- Big question.

## Visual Language

- Characters are glossy, smooth, white, featureless doll figures.
- The whole head is white and blank, not a theatrical mask.
- No eyes, no eye holes, no mouth, no nose, no facial details.
- Clothing must match the role: suits, coats, prison clothes, military uniforms, police gear, workwear, hats, helmets, gloves, boots.
- Props and rooms must carry the story: files, sealed envelopes, courtrooms, border fences, offices, empty corridors, train platforms, prison yards, control rooms, desks, lamps, briefcases.
- Avoid generated readable text inside AI clips. All readable text must be added by our renderer.
- Camera language: slow push-ins, controlled lateral moves, low-angle wide shots, closeups of hands, ominous stillness, surveillance-like framing.
- Vary shot purpose: reenactment, surveillance observation, real archival evidence, object closeup, map-like spatial context, dossier-style reveal, and looming scale shot.
- At least one beat in a 60-120 second intro should feel like evidence rather than cinema: archival photo, public domain image, official object, real place, or treated still.
- For every 90 seconds, avoid a wall of slow cinematic tableaus:
  - maximum 35% slow cinematic tableau
  - at least 25% action or process shots
  - at least 15% archival/evidence inserts for real topics
  - at least 10% object/detail/motion-graphic style shots
  - climax beats must visibly move
- Be hyper-specific in video prompts: shot type, camera angle, camera movement, foreground action, background environment, wardrobe, props, lighting, lens/shot distance, mood, and what physically moves.
- Good p-video action/process examples: typing, plugging in a cable, walking into court, lawyers opening briefcases, hands removing a screw, police entering a hallway, server rack lights blinking, camera pushing past evidence, figure running through rain.

## Overlay Text

- Use restraint. Most beats should have only one big punch overlay, or none if the visual is already strong.
- Do not turn narration into subtitles. The overlays are attention weapons, not transcription.
- Overlay text should usually be 1-3 words.
- It should be a phrase the narrator actually says, except chapter cards.
- Big punch overlays must be self-contained hooks, not sentence fragments. Good: "THE FLAW", "NO WITNESSES", "32 DOLLARS", "THE LOOPHOLE". Bad: "WAS ABOUT TO", "SENT IT TO", "THE MAN WHO".
- Never end a punch overlay on connector/filler words: to, of, the, a, an, and, or, but, with, for, from, by, in, on, at, into, over, under, before, after, because, that, who, what, when, where, why, how.
- Do not split across a pause, comma, period, question mark, exclamation mark, semicolon, colon, or dash. If the narrator naturally pauses, the overlay must stop before the pause.
- Prefer nouns, names, numbers, places, accusations, consequences, and clean repeated clauses over partial sentence chunks.
- Repeated phrasing gets separate popups:
  - NO GUARDS
  - NO WITNESSES
  - NO INMATES
- Dates and locations are mandatory when spoken:
  - "13th September 1934" -> "13TH SEPTEMBER 1934"
  - "Spring 1979" -> "SPRING 1979"
  - "January 2011" -> "JANUARY 2011"
  - "Lisbon, Portugal" -> "LISBON", then "PORTUGAL"
- Date text is a special fixed style: top-left corner, smaller than punch text, yellow serif glow, no dark screen dim, typewriter reveal, reverse typewriter exit. Type the full date as one label. Never split the year into a second date overlay.
- Location text is a special fixed style: bottom-right corner, smaller than punch text, green/yellow serif glow, no dark screen dim, typewriter reveal, reverse typewriter exit.
- Date and location labels may linger roughly two seconds after the spoken words, then exit with reverse typewriter and no exit sound.
- Text must remain visible: use safe positions, avoid edges, avoid covering important faces/hands when possible.
- If a phrase repeats, do not make fragments like "NO ONE" unless that is the whole spoken phrase. Prefer the full repeated clause or a clean compressed phrase that still lands on the right spoken moment.
- For every overlay, decide whether the background should darken. Use dimming for huge punch text, date reveals, danger sequences, and chapter cards when readability needs it. Do not dim the screen for bottom location labels.

## Fonts Available

Choose only from:

- Impact: huge block words, threats, danger, reveals.
- Arial Black: clean bold blocks.
- Bahnschrift Condensed: modern compressed labels.
- Franklin Gothic Heavy: bold documentary labels.
- Georgia: glowing serif mystery text.
- Palatino Linotype: elegant glowing person names and chapter cards.
- Book Antiqua: old-document serif labels.
- Cambria: formal/legal serif labels.
- Times New Roman: formal/legal chapter cards.
- Trebuchet MS: smaller locations or UI-like labels.

Use the George-Hotz-style glowing serif treatment for names, places, and precise evidence labels. It can be green or yellow. Use huge yellow block text only for rare impact moments.

## Color Palette

- Bright green: digital, legal, system, money, access, secrets.
- Bright yellow: date, mystery, evidence, formal chapter cards.
- Bright red: danger, violence, failure, arrest, disaster, "no witnesses" style sequences.
- White: small chapter prefix, location prefix, or minor label.

## Overlay Effects

- pop: fast scale-in, camera click.
- typewriter: letters appear one by one while a short chopped typing sound plays during the entry animation.
- glitch: unstable title/card text with flicker and light pulse.
- fade: slow reveal, used sparingly.

## Transitions

- hard_cut: normal pacing.
- dip_black: chapter shift or ominous reveal.
- flash_click: image/text impact beat.
- glitch_cut: tech/state/secrecy transition.
- crossfade: rare, only for time passing or memory-like moments.

## Sound Design

- Use low background ambience under most beats.
- Use camera clicks on pop overlays.
- Use typewriter ticks on date and location overlays.
- Use risers before impossible reveals.
- Use low booms for chapter cards or huge consequences.
- Suggested ambient beds:
  - low_drone
  - room_tone
  - industrial_hum
  - surveillance_noise
  - distant_wind
  - courthouse_room
- If you want an ambience not in the list, pick the closest available ambience and describe the desired mood in the beat title or visual prompt.
- Ambience should be felt, not heard. Narration must remain dominant.
- Available local SFX assets are camera_click, typewriter/typewriter_tick, glitch/glitch_burst, boom, and whoosh. Prefer those before asking for anything custom.
- If a custom sound is needed, ask for a short generic query like "metal door slam", "camera shutter", "paper stamp", or "electric glitch". Do not request rare exact sounds.
- If a requested custom sound is not available from local assets or safe CC0/public-domain downloads, fall back to the closest local SFX or no sound. Do not make a scene depend on a rare custom sound.
- Big overlays should usually have one sound cue. Date and location labels get typewriter ticks. Do not add a separate sound cue for every word.
- Camera-click pop overlays are for character names, decisive evidence, consequences, impossible turns, and repeated story phrases only.
- Choose ambience deliberately for every beat. Use known ambience names when possible. If asking for custom ambience, keep it generic and downloadable, like "cold wind ambience", "courtroom room tone", or "low tension drone".

## Archival / Real Image Inserts

- Use real images sparingly for named real people, historical places, official objects, courtroom photos, public buildings, screenshots, or artifacts.
- A real image insert should feel dynamic: slow zoom, parallax, slight handheld drift, scanlines, chromatic aberration, dark vignette, and a subtle click/glitch when it appears.
- Archival images should be graded into the video world: old TV/CRT feel, scanlines, vignette, low saturation, noisy evidence-monitor look, not clean raw images.
- Never leave a real image static on screen.
- If a main character exists, plan at least one archival image insert when legally and practically available.
- If the story is about a real place or landmark, plan at least one archival insert in a 60-120 second intro when a safe image likely exists.
- Use archival inserts as credibility hits: the viewer should feel "this is real" after a cinematic reenactment setup.
- Real images should come from safe sources: Wikimedia Commons, official government/company pages, public domain archives, licensed stock, or user-provided assets.
- If no safe real image is available, use a generated reenactment instead and do not invent a fake photo.
- Do not use copyrighted news photos unless the user provides rights or accepts the risk.
- If an archival image is unavailable, any sound effect, transition, or overlay that exists only because of that image must be skipped too. No orphan image-click sounds.

## Beat Direction

- A short intro should alternate between cinematic reenactment and credibility inserts.
- Early beats can be still, ominous, and precise. Climax beats need more motion: push-ins, tilts, lateral tracking, flag movement, surveillance zoom, or archival drift.
- Do not make every generated clip equally cinematic. Some shots should feel like observation, surveillance, file review, or archival evidence.
- For each beat ask: what is the viewer learning, what should they feel, and what is the single strongest visual proof or metaphor?
- Avoid obvious repetition. If one beat is a wide cinematic shot, the next should likely be a closeup, archival insert, surveillance angle, object detail, or motion-heavy reveal.
- Use transitions with intent:
  - hard_cut for pace
  - flash_click for a photo/evidence reveal
  - glitch_cut for state secrecy or technical deception
  - dip_black for ominous turns

## Do Not Do

- Do not ask AI video generation to create readable signs, posters, documents, subtitles, labels, screens, or title cards.
- Do not make characters naked, skin-colored, anatomical, or display-mannequin-like.
- Do not make overlays long sentences.
- Do not make overlays that are sentence leftovers ending in connector words like "to", "of", "who", or "that".
- Do not use chapters in a short intro-only test.
- Do not use text placement that leaves words cropped or unreadable.
